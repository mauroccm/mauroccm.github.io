The Climate Connection

Solving the Puzzle of Yellow Fever's Unprecedented Spread

A project to build an early-warning system by understanding how climate change creates new pathways for disease.

The Problem

A Historic Outbreak in a New Region

Between 2016 and 2022, Brazil faced the largest Yellow Fever outbreak in 80 years. The virus didn't just stay in its usual Amazon home. It spread over 1,500 km into the country's most densely populated region, the Atlantic Forest, exposing millions of unvaccinated people to a deadly threat.

Image: A map showing the historic Amazonian (green) and Cerrado (yellow) biomes, and the new spread into the Atlantic Forest (dark green) region.

The Mystery

How Did the Virus Survive Winter?

This spread shouldn't have been possible. The virus is transmitted by mosquitoes, and both the virus and mosquitoes die off in cold weather.

Temperatures below 16°C (61°F) are a natural firewall that stops transmission. The virus's path included high-altitude regions that are normally too cold in winter.

So, what changed to let the virus pass?

Image: This graph shows how mosquito vector capacity—the ability to transmit the virus—plummets at low temperatures.

Our Hypothesis

The "Thermal Corridor" Effect

We believe the outbreak was enabled by anomalous climatic conditions, likely linked to the 2015-2016 El Niño event.

These anomalies created "Thermal Corridors"—brief periods of unusually warm winter weather in high-altitude areas. These warm spells acted as a "bridge," allowing the virus and its mosquito vectors to survive, cross the cold barrier, and invade new, susceptible regions.

Our Plan

Four Objectives to Find the Answer

To prove this hypothesis and build a solution, our project is built on four key objectives.

1. Integrate Data

Build a massive geo-database combining climate records, human cases, primate infections, and environmental data.

2. Analyze El Niño

Statistically link the El Niño (ENSO) phenomenon to the specific local temperature and rain anomalies that formed the "corridors."

3. Model the "Corridors"

Use spatio-temporal modeling to identify the *exact* minimum temperatures and conditions that allowed the virus to spread.

4. Build a Risk Model

Create a validated predictive model that uses key climate data to forecast the *probability and direction* of future outbreaks.

The Payoff & Impact

A Predictive Early-Warning System

This research is not just academic. The final objective is a concrete tool for public health.

By understanding the specific climate triggers, we can create an early-warning system that uses weather forecasts to predict outbreak risk months in advance. This will give public health officials the critical lead time needed to deploy vaccination campaigns, target vector control, and save lives—all before the first case is even reported.
