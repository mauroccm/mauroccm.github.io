The Temperature-Virus Link

Yellow Fever's Extrinsic Incubation Period (EIP)

The Extrinsic Incubation Period (EIP) is the critical time required for a mosquito to become infectious after ingesting a virus. For Yellow Fever, this period is not fixed; it is highly dependent on ambient temperature. This infographic visualizes data from key literature to illustrate this relationship.

EIP Shortens as Temperature Rises

This chart synthesizes data from multiple studies, showing a strong inverse relationship: as temperatures increase from 22°C to 32°C, the median EIP for Yellow Fever virus in *Aedes* mosquitoes drops dramatically from over two weeks to just a few days.

The Transmission Trade-Off

While warmer temperatures shorten the EIP, there is a trade-off between virus development and mosquito survival. This balance creates an "optimal" range for transmission.

Too Cold (< 20°C)

Virus replication is extremely slow. The EIP becomes so long that the mosquito is likely to die before it can ever become infectious. Transmission is unlikely.

Optimal Range (~24°C - 30°C)

This range provides a "sweet spot" where the EIP is short enough (e.g., 7-12 days) and mosquito survival rates remain high, maximizing the potential for virus transmission.

Too Hot (> 32°C)

The EIP becomes very short (e.g., ~4 days), but this advantage is offset by increased mosquito mortality. Extreme heat stress reduces the mosquito's lifespan, potentially limiting transmission.
